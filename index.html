00 — The Data

12,901 sales, matched at the address level, checked by coordinate

Every sale in this report is matched from three sources: MLS transaction records, HMDA mortgage disclosures, and Data Axle's mover file, joined on normalized street address. Address matching is inherently fuzzy in that no two data providers format "715 N. Mesa St." identically so every match carries a confidence score, and this report only draws conclusions from matches strong enough to trust.

Where the line is drawn

7,544 records (58.5%) sit at 85% confidence or higher — a near-exact address match, and the base for every buyer-level finding in this report. Checking a sample of these against independently geocoded coordinates confirms the same physical property in roughly 85 of every 100 cases at this threshold, rising to nearly 90 of 100 above 95%. Below 85%, agreement falls off fast enough that those records are treated as directional color, not counted evidence.

01 — The Compass

El Paso moves southeast, east, and northeast but not evenly in every direction

For every local buyer, Data Axle records the exact latitude and longitude of their prior residence and their new one. Drawing a line between those two points gives a precise location for origin and destination for 8,483 in-metro moves.

Southeast alone accounts for more moves than west, southwest, and south combined.

The clearest single number in this dataset

Southeast is the single most common bearing for a move in El Paso. 18.7% of all in-metro relocations head that direction, ahead of east (14.9%) and northeast (13.7%). Together, the eastward arc (E, NE, SE) accounts for 47.3% of every move traced, nearly half the market pointed in one general direction, while the westward arc (W, SW, NW) totals just 30.9%. This isn't a story about people leaving downtown for "the suburbs" in the abstract. Instead, it's a specific, measurable pull toward the east and southeast quadrant of the metro.

02 — Distance & Price

How far someone moves says almost nothing about what they pay

The median local move covers 4.3 miles (interquartile range: 1.9 to 8.4 miles), represent moves of short hops, not long treks. What's more surprising is what distance doesn't predict.

Median sale price and buyer income by distance moved, n=8,483
A near-zero relationship

The correlation between distance moved and sale price is -0.004; between distance moved and buyer income, -0.018. Both values are statistically indistinguishable from zero: a buyer who relocates half a mile pays a median $259,999; one who relocates 15-50 miles pays $279,950. This is a modest difference that doesn't scale with distance at all in between (buyers moving 7-15 miles actually pay the least, $255,000). Whatever is driving home prices in El Paso, it isn't how far the buyer traveled to get there.

03 — The Fort Bliss Effect

Nearly a third of every home loan in this data is VA-backed

Nationally, VA loans make up roughly 8-10% of home purchase mortgages. In this data, they're 31.9%; a rate roughly three times the national norm and the clearest fingerprint Fort Bliss leaves on the housing market.

Loan type distribution, HMDA-matched purchases, n=4,341
Where VA-financed purchases land, by region, n=1,384
VA buyers are concentrated exactly where the new construction is

75.5% of all VA-financed purchases land in just two regions: the Far East growth corridor (46.6%) and the Northeast, adjacent to Fort Bliss itself (28.9%). VA buyers skew young — 78.9% are under 45 — and buy at a median price of $285,000, squarely in the same entry-level price band as the broader workforce-housing story in this market. Buyers coming from out of state use a VA loan 35.7% of the time, versus 30.6% for local buyers. This is a meaningful share of what looks like "out-of-state migration" in this data is very plausibly a military reassignment, not a discretionary economic relocation.

04 — Neighborhood Mix

Buyers from every origin land in similar neighborhoods

HMDA attaches each loan to census-tract context: how a tract's median family income compares to the metro as a whole, and what share of its population is a racial or ethnic minority. Comparing these across buyer origin tests whether different groups are sorting into systematically different types of neighborhoods.

What the data shows

Local and out-of-state buyers land in tracts with an identical median income ratio (124% of the metro median — both groups are, if anything, buying into above-average-income tracts) and nearly identical minority population share (89.2% vs. 88.1% — El Paso's tracts run heavily Hispanic/minority citywide, and that doesn't change by who's buying). Regional New Mexico buyers are the one group that stands out, landing in noticeably lower-income tracts (109% ratio) — consistent with New Mexico buyers more often purchasing in the Lower Valley and West side rather than the pricier growth corridor.

05 — First-Time Buyers

The real first-time buyer is defined by tenure, not age

Data Axle flags roughly a quarter of buyers with a direct tenure signal at their prior address: confirmed homeowner, probable homeowner, or "not a home owner." That last group is the closest thing in this data to a genuine first-time-buyer flag, since it describes what the person's living situation actually was before the sale — not an assumption based on their age.

What the data shows

Confirmed first-time buyers purchase homes at a median $55,000 below repeat buyers; a real, sensible gap that a straightforward age cutoff would miss entirely. This tenure-confirmed group isn't concentrated in the under-30 crowd. More of them are 35-44 than 25-34, meaning a large share of El Paso's true first-time buyers are simply people who spent longer renting, not people buying unusually young.

Reading This Report

What this data can and can't establish

  • The compass and distance analysis covers in-metro moves only (≤50 miles) with valid, non-placeholder coordinates on both ends, representing 8,483 of 12,901 total sales.
  • Neighborhood-context and loan-type figures are drawn from the 4,341 sales that resolved to an HMDA record (33.6% of all sales). Cash purchases and unmatched loans aren't represented in those sections.
  • The tenure-based first-time-buyer flag covers about a quarter of buyers (3,231 of 12,901).
  • VA-loan geography and the military-relocation reading are inferential, not observed. HMDA records loan type, not military status directly; the concentration and demographic pattern make the connection to Fort Bliss highly plausible, not certain.
  • Neighborhood income/minority comparisons are medians across origin groups. They describe the typical buyer's destination, not the full distribution, and don't control for price point or timing.
