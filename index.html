El Paso Housing Market Matrix

An empirical synthesis of MLS transactions, HMDA credit registries, and demographic profiles exposing structural disparities in the El Paso housing market.

Establishing the Baseline

This report translates a complex, multi-stage data linkage pipeline into actionable market intelligence. Historically, analyzing the municipal housing market has been hindered by fractured datasets. Local transaction logs (MLS) detail physical property attributes and market prices, while federal credit registries (HMDA) track the demographic profiles and financial leverage of the buyers. Because these datasets lack a shared unique identifier (like an institutional lender name), they existed in silos.

To overcome this, an exploratory pipeline was constructed in Google Colab. By adapting the econometric framework established by Avenancio-León & Howard (2022), the pipeline utilizes spatial anchoring (Census Tracts), financial signatures (Sale Price vs. Property Value), and probabilistic demographic mapping (Data Axle Mover profiles) to stitch these records together.

Key Analytical Objectives

This synthesis isolates three critical phenomena currently destabilizing the local market:

  • The spatial polarization of capital (Core Stagnation vs. Suburban Proliferation).
  • The distinct financial friction profiles (Underwriting Blockades vs. Leverage Traps).
  • The structural failure of current municipal infill subsidies to address local credit realities.

Data Integrity

Multi-Stage Record Linkage Pipelines

The foundation of this analysis rests on a probabilistic record linkage script. To replace the missing lender identifier required for deterministic joins, the system employs an aggressive text cleaning function and fuzzy string matching via RapidFuzz to align physical transaction addresses with credit profiles.

Matches are classified into certainty tiers based on their alignment scores and corroborated against financial signatures (e.g., matching MLS Sale Price to HMDA Property Value within a tight ±1% tolerance) before incorporating demographic overlays.

Methodology Takeaways

  • Score 100: Absolute exact character alignment (Tier 1).
  • Score 85–99: High-certainty spelling or directional variations.
  • Validation: All-cash sales are programmatically excluded as they bypass the credit registry.
Geographic Polarization

Spatial Flight & Suburban Proliferation

The merged transaction footprint exposes a severe spatial divide across El Paso County. The data details a coordinated, systemic outward migration vector from the urban interior toward the suburban perimeter.

Driven primarily by younger buyer cohorts seeking modern, multi-bedroom configurations, capital is fleeing the historic core. The resulting transaction volume is highly asymmetrical, threatening the long-term sustainability of the central tax base.

Footprint Reality Check

  • Far East Montana Corridor (79938): Acts as a massive demand collector, processing 5,194 captured records.
  • Central Urban Core: Remains structurally stagnant, registering a baseline of only 643 active transaction records.
Financial Friction Points

The Central Core Underwriting Blockade

Inside central census tracts (e.g., Tract 48141010502), market demand is suffocated not by a lack of buyer interest, but by severe institutional underwriting friction. Local residents are attempting to secure highly modest loans—averaging just $195,277.78.

Despite these conservative targets, applicants face strict programmatic rejections. The HMDA credit registry data reveals a distinct hierarchy of root causes preventing core stabilization, proving that zoning changes alone cannot cure the population drain.

Root-Cause Hierarchy (Tract 48141010502)

  • Rank 1: Credit History. The absolute leading driver of application failure.
  • Rank 2: Debt-to-Income (DTI). Localized wages are insufficient relative to existing debt burdens.
  • Rank 3: Collateral Flaws. Bank appraisals fail due to deferred maintenance on historic stock.
Market Drivers

The Middle-Class Engine & Demographic Squeeze

Cross-referencing Data Axle profiles isolates the true economic engine of the county: local working families earning between $90,000 and $120,000. This bracket dominates transaction volume within the competitive $250,000 to $350,000 price tiers, particularly among the 25–44 age cohorts.

However, filtering mechanisms have broken down. Long-term seniors (over 65) are locked into the core due to property tax reset penalties if they move. This blocks older starter homes from cycling back to the market, forcing young families under 25 into an acute supply shortage, while out-of-town inflows arrive with higher incomes that shift price ceilings upward.

The Suburban Leverage Trap

Families fleeing the core for the Far East (79938) are buying ~$263k homes on ~$92.5k median incomes. This compresses their DTI tolerances, pushing household leverage to structural limits and increasing vulnerability to macroeconomic shocks.

Policy Evaluation

Structural Mismatches: The Infill Reality

Evaluating existing municipal interventions against the data reveals severe misalignments. Under the city's Title 20 infill framework, rental units are subject to a 5-year property tax abatement with no corresponding price caps or affordability restrictions on for-sale residential units.

Consequently, developers utilize extensive public tax rebates to construct for-sale infill homes in the core that list at or above $300,000. This completely overshoots the financial capacity of local residents.

The Empirical Gap

The localized core credit baseline (the max loan capacity before rejection) sits at ~$195,000. Subsidizing $300k+ builds does not serve the existing neighborhood; rather, it clears the path for out-of-town capital and accelerates core displacement.
